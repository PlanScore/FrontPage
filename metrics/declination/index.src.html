Declination

A gerrymander purposefully designs districts around the win/loss threshold to ensure that more districts of the favored party end up on the winning side. A neutral map, by contrast, designs the districts for other reasons and does not treat the win/loss threshold as particularly special. Declination uses this fact by capturing asymmetry around the threshold, as in the plot below.

The declination metric treats asymmetry in the distribution of votes across districts as indicative of partisan bias in a districting plan. To calculate declination, we take the angles of the lines between each party’s mean vote share in the districts they won and the point on the 50% line between the mass of points representing each party. We then take the difference between those two angles and divide by π/2 to convert the result from radians to fractions of 90 degrees. We then do a further adjustment to account for differences in the number of seats across legislative chambers.

For more about declination, see Gregory Warrington's Election Law Journal article, Quantifying Gerrymandering Using the Vote Distribution and paper Introduction to the Declination Function for Gerrymanders.
