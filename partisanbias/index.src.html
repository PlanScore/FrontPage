Partisan Bias

Partisan Bias (PB) shows how much a system unfairly favors a single party, comparing the number of votes cast in an election to the number of legislative seats won. A high PB score results when one party is able to consistently convert votes into district victories at a higher rate than they would under a symmetrical plan, even winning a majority of districts despite earning half the votes in a competitive 50/50 election.

The PB score was proposed by Andrew Gelman and Gary King in their 1994 American Journal of Political Science paper, A Unified Method of Evaluating Electoral Systems and Redistricting Plans.
