What is Partisan Gerrymandering?

It's not just about crazy shapes

Partisan gerrymandering is a deeply undemocratic practice. It produces state legislatures and congressional delegations that look nothing like the voters they are meant to represent. The basic notion that in a democracy, the people rule, is thus subverted. In a gerrymandered state, it is the will of the mapmaking party that governs.

How Do We Measure It?

We used three metrics to examine the bias in these plans. These are the three metrics. When they all agree that the plan is biased, that's a pretty good sign that the plan is biased.

  • Efficiency Gap
  • Mean/ Median
  • Partisan Bias

Efficiency Gap (EG) shows relative wasted votes between parties, comparing the total statewide number of superfluous and losing votes for each party. A high EG score happens when one party’s votes are wasted at a higher rate than the other’s, forcing them to earn more votes to win the same number of districts as they would under a fair plan. The EG score counts two kinds of wasted votes under a plan: those in excess of 51% for a district winner and those for a district loser.

The EG metric was proposed by Nicholas O. Stephanopoulos and Eric M. McGhee in their 2015 University of Chicago Law Review paper, Partisan Gerrymandering and the Efficiency Gap.

Mean/Median (MM) shows partisan packing by a majority party, comparing the party’s vote share in the state’s deciding median district to its overall state share. A large MM score suggest potential harm, but it may also happen naturally. Actual harm can be shown where the MM score shows a party consistently winning a majority of votes but failing to win a majority of districts.

The MM score was proposed by Michael D. McDonald and Robin E. Best in their 2015 Election Law Journal article, Unfair Partisan Gerrymanders in Politics and Law: A Diagnostic Applied to Six Cases.

Partisan Bias (PB) shows how much a system unfairly favors a single party, comparing the number of votes cast in an election to the number of legislative seats won. A high PB score results when one party is able to consistently convert votes into district victories at a higher rate than they would under a symmetrical plan, even winning a majority of districts despite earning half the votes in a competitive 50/50 election.

The PB score was proposed by Andrew Gelman and Gary King in their 1994 American Journal of Political Science paper, A Unified Method of Evaluating Electoral Systems and Redistricting Plans.
